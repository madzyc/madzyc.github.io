Pressure at the Pump: How a War in the Middle East is Raising Prices for Americans

By Maddy Cox


Though actual conflict is taking place far overseas in the Middle East, many Americans are feeling the effects as close as their local gas stations. In Syracuse alone, the current average price of regular unleaded gas sits at about $4.171, over a dollar and ten cents more expensive than just a year ago. Since so much of the world's crude oil comes from the Middle East, the conflict in the area over the past couple years, and now the conflict with the US has not only impacted the collection of crude oil, but the transportation. Warfare in the region has damaged necessary infrastructure in fracking areas. The Strait of Hormuz, which according to NPR, is a waterway through which over 20% of the entire world's crude oil passes, has also been used as a leverage tactic in more recent ceasefire negotiations between Iran and the US. Because of this, ships have been unable to pass through consistently, creating disruptions in the trade of oil and causing crude oil prices to rise. Prior to the start of the war, crude oil cost around $70 a barrel, but since the start of the conflict, prices have risen as high as $120 a barrel, and now sit at about $100. The rise in price of crude oil is directly felt by consumers as the cost of gas rises along with it. Across the US, gas prices naturally fluctuate, with the highest costs typically along the West Coast and Mid-Atlantic region. While these fluctuations occur constantly, it means that some states find themselves paying even higher prices than their neighbors.


New York is no exception to the national trend of rising gas prices. In fact, in the past week alone, New York has seen a 25 cent jump in gas prices. While much of this jump does relate back to conflict in the Middle East, another cause is they May 1st requirement for gas stations to switch to their “summer blend.” Summer gas blends are created with the ultimate goal of reducing air pollution and smog by nipping the problem in the bud and decreasing fuel evaporation at the vehicle level. Though great for the environment, and often more efficient for the vehicles themselves, this blend costs more money to make, and can increase gas prices by around 15 cents per gallon.

While gas stations within Syracuse find themselves within cents of each other trying to remain competitive, the real difference is in the price of gas in Syracuse today versus a year ago.

As of April 8, a ceasefire between the US and Iran is in place, but tensions between the two regions remain tense. Despite the ceasefire, sporadic warfare is still taking place in the Middle East, Israel, and neighboring regions. Many US troops remain in the Middle East and active conflict zones, with airstrikes on ships and forced boarding of ships still taking place. Though gas prices have eased up from their $120 a barrel peak about a week after the start of the conflict, prices still remain higher than what many Americans are used to. After weeks of these raised prices, many Americans are complaining about how they’ve felt the pressure on their pockets as they have to fill up their tanks.
